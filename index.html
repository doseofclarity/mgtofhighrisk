Conditions in Pregnancy

Comprehensive evidence‑based management of hypertensive disorders, diabetes, preterm labor, bleeding conditions, and multiple gestation. Master risk stratification, pharmacologic and non‑pharmacologic interventions, and nursing surveillance.

Overview & Risk Stratification

A high‑risk pregnancy is one in which the mother, fetus, or newborn has a significantly increased chance of morbidity or mortality. Management involves early identification, specialized surveillance, and timely intervention.
  • Risk factors: Maternal age (<18 or >35), chronic medical conditions (HTN, DM), obstetrical history (prior preterm, preeclampsia), multiple gestation, genetic disorders, substance use.
  • Levels of risk: Low, moderate, high. High‑risk pregnancies require multidisciplinary care (MFM, neonatology, anesthesia).
  • General management principles: Frequent prenatal visits, targeted screening, fetal surveillance (NST, BPP), management of underlying conditions, planning for delivery timing and mode.

Hypertensive Disorders (PIH, Preeclampsia, Eclampsia)

  • Gestational hypertension: BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks, no proteinuria. Monitor; treat if severe.
  • Preeclampsia: Hypertension + proteinuria or end‑organ dysfunction. Management: Antihypertensives (labetalol, nifedipine, hydralazine), magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis, delivery at 37 weeks (or earlier if severe).
  • Eclampsia: Preeclampsia with grand‑mal seizures. Immediate magnesium sulfate, control BP, deliver after stabilization.
  • HELLP syndrome: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets. Requires prompt delivery, corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity, platelet transfusion if <20,000.
  • Nursing interventions: BP monitoring, urine protein, reflex assessment, fetal surveillance, seizure precautions, IV magnesium monitoring (respiratory rate, DTR, urine output).

Diabetes in Pregnancy (Pregestational & GDM)

  • Management goals: Fasting glucose <95 mg/dL, 1h postprandial <140 mg/dL, 2h <120 mg/dL.
  • Medical nutrition therapy: Consistent carbohydrate, small frequent meals, exercise.
  • Insulin therapy: Lispro/aspart (prandial), NPH (basal). Insulin does not cross placenta. Metformin and glyburide used but with caution.
  • Fetal surveillance: Ultrasound for growth, NST/BPP starting at 32–34 weeks, especially if on medication.
  • Delivery timing: Well‑controlled GDM at 39–40 weeks; poorly controlled or pregestational DM earlier (37–39 weeks). Intrapartum insulin drip to maintain glucose 70–110 mg/dL.
  • Postpartum: Glucose tolerance test at 6–12 weeks; breastfeeding encouraged (reduces future DM risk).

Preterm Labor & PPROM

  • Diagnosis: Regular uterine contractions with cervical change between 20–36⁶⁄⁷ weeks.
  • Tocolysis: Nifedipine, indomethacin, or terbutaline to delay delivery for 48 hours (for corticosteroids). Magnesium sulfate no longer recommended for tocolysis but for fetal neuroprotection.
  • Corticosteroids: Betamethasone or dexamethasone (24–34 weeks) to accelerate fetal lung maturity.
  • Preterm PROM (PPROM): Rupture before 37 weeks without labor. Manage with antibiotics (latency), corticosteroids, and delivery by 34 weeks.
  • Nursing care: Monitor contractions, FHR, signs of infection (chorioamnionitis), administer tocolytics and corticosteroids, prepare for possible delivery.

Bleeding Disorders (Previa/Abruption) & Multiple Gestation

  • Placenta previa: Painless bright red bleeding. Management: Pelvic rest, hospitalization if bleeding, corticosteroids, cesarean delivery at 36–37 weeks.
  • Placental abruption: Painful dark bleeding, uterine tenderness. Management: Immediate assessment, IV access, blood transfusion, emergency delivery (vaginal or cesarean depending on stability).
  • Multiple gestation: Increased risk of preterm labor, preeclampsia, GDM, IUGR, malpresentation. Management: More frequent surveillance, cervical length monitoring, antenatal corticosteroids, planned cesarean for non‑vertex presenting twin.

Nursing Management & Fetal Surveillance

  • Fetal surveillance methods: Non‑stress test (NST), biophysical profile (BPP), contraction stress test (CST), umbilical artery Doppler.
  • Antepartum testing indications: Hypertensive disorders, diabetes, multiple gestation, IUGR, preterm PROM, previous stillbirth.
  • Nursing role: Educate patient on kick counts, schedule testing, interpret results (reactive NST = reassuring), recognize non‑reassuring patterns, coordinate with provider.
  • Maternal monitoring: Vital signs, urine protein, weight gain, symptoms (headache, visual changes, epigastric pain).
  • Psychosocial support: High‑risk patients experience anxiety, fear, and possible role restriction. Provide emotional support, clear communication, and involve family.
